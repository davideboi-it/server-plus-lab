# 1.4 Network Cabling and Transceivers

## 1. Copper Cabling (Twisted Pair)
Copper cables transmit electrical signals. For modern servers, you must know the current standards, shielding types, and fire safety ratings.

### Categories and Speeds
* **Cat 5e:** Speeds up to 1 Gbps at 100 meters.
* **Cat 6:** Speeds up to 10 Gbps, but with a strict distance limit of 55 meters for 10G speeds.
* **Cat 6a:** Speeds up to 10 Gbps at the full standard distance of 100 meters.
* **Connector:** Copper ethernet networks universally use the **RJ-45** connector.

### Shielding and Fire Safety
* **UTP vs. STP:** * **UTP (Unshielded Twisted Pair):** The standard, cost-effective cable for normal environments.
  * **STP (Shielded Twisted Pair):** Contains internal metal shielding to protect data from severe EMI (Electromagnetic Interference) often found near heavy machinery or dense power cables.
* **Plenum vs. Non-Plenum (PVC):** Plenum-grade cables use a special fire-retardant jacket (low-smoke FEP or PVC). They are legally and strictly required when running cables through plenum spaces (like drop ceilings or raised floors used for HVAC air returns) to prevent the spread of toxic fumes during a fire.

## 2. Fiber Optic Cabling

Fiber carries light pulses instead of electrical signals, making it completely immune to EMI and capable of massive bandwidth over long distances.

### Fiber Modes
* **Multimode (MMF):** Has a wider core (50 or 62.5 microns) that allows multiple light beams to travel simultaneously by bouncing off the walls. It is cheaper and primarily used for short distances like LANs or within the Data Center. MMF jackets are typically Aqua or Orange.
* **Single Mode (SMF):** Has a very narrow core (8-10 microns) and uses a single, direct laser beam. It is used for long-distance runs (up to 80-200 km). SMF patch cables always use a Yellow jacket.

### Fiber Connectors to Memorize
* **LC:** "Lock and Click" or "Little Connector". A small form factor connector with a retaining tab. Currently the most common connector in data centers.
* **SC:** "Stick and Click" or "Square Connector". A square, push-pull connector.
* **ST:** "Stick and Twist". A legacy round connector that locks using a BNC-style bayonet mount.
* **MPO / MTP:** High-density connectors that can terminate 12 to 24 fibers in a single plug. Required for 40 Gbps and 100 Gbps QSFP breakout connections.

## 3. Transceivers and Direct Attach Cables
Transceivers are the hot-pluggable modules inserted into empty switch or server ports to interface with different cable types (copper or fiber).

### Transceiver Form Factors
* **SFP (Small Form-factor Pluggable):** Supports standard Ethernet speeds of 1 Gbps (and up to 4.25 Gbps for Fibre Channel).
* **SFP+:** Enhanced version supporting 10 Gbps. *(Note: SFP+ ports can often accept 1G SFP modules, but SFP+ modules cannot be used in older 1G SFP ports).*
* **QSFP / QSFP+ (Quad SFP):** Supports 40 Gbps. It features four dedicated 10 Gbps lanes and can "break out" into 4 separate 10 Gbps connections.
* **QSFP28:** Designed for 100 Gbps data rates (four 25 Gbps lanes).

### DAC and AOC Cables
* **DAC (Direct Attach Copper):** Uses Twinax copper cabling with factory-terminated transceiver modules (like SFP+) permanently attached at both ends. It is a low-priced, ultra-low latency alternative to fiber, heavily used for short Top-of-Rack (ToR) switch-to-server connections.
* **AOC (Active Optical Cable):** The fiber optic equivalent of a DAC. It has transceivers permanently attached to a fiber cable, allowing for longer runs and thinner cables than DAC, but at a higher cost.

## 4. Network Management and Redundancy
* **NIC Teaming / Bonding:** Grouping multiple physical Network Interface Cards (NICs) into a single logical interface. This provides load balancing and failover if a cable or port dies. 
  * **LACP (Link Aggregation Control Protocol / 802.3ad):** The standard protocol used by switches to negotiate and manage these bundled links.
* **Path Diversity:** When running redundant links, always avoid routing them through the exact same physical path, conduit, or tray to prevent a single physical accident (like a cut cable) from taking down both connections.
* **Labeling:** Proper cable management requires labeling cables at both ends to facilitate rapid troubleshooting. The standard for telecom cabling administration is **ANSI/TIA-606**.
